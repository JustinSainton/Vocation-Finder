# **VOCATIONAL DISCERNMENT ASSESSMENT \- 20 QUESTIONS**

## ***Revised for Ages 17-21 (Juniors/Seniors & Early College)***

---

## **CATEGORY 1: SERVICE ORIENTATION (3 questions)**

**Question 1:** Think about a time when you helped someone and it actually felt meaningful—not just because you were supposed to, but because you genuinely wanted to. What did you do? What made you want to help in that way?

**Question 2:** Imagine a friend comes to you with a problem. What kind of problem would make you think, "I actually want to help with this"? Describe the situation and what you'd naturally want to do to help.

**Question 3:** When you think about making a difference in someone's life, what does that look like? Describe a specific way you'd want to help people—be as detailed as possible.

---

## **CATEGORY 2: PROBLEM-SOLVING DRAW (3 questions)**

**Question 4:** What's something in the world—big or small—that really bothers you or makes you frustrated? It could be something in your school, your community, or society in general. What is it, and why does it bother you so much?

**Question 5:** If you could fix one thing about how the world works, what would it be? Describe what's broken and what you wish was different.

**Question 6:** Think about a problem you've noticed that most people seem to ignore or accept as normal, but you can't stop thinking about. What is it, and why can't you let it go?

---

## **CATEGORY 3: ENERGY & ENGAGEMENT (4 questions)**

**Question 7:** Describe a time when you were working on something—a project, activity, hobby, anything—and you completely lost track of time. What were you doing? What made it so absorbing?

**Question 8:** Think about a class, project, or activity that actually energized you instead of draining you. What were you doing? What made it feel different from other things?

**Question 9:** What's something you've made, built, organized, or accomplished that you're genuinely proud of? Describe what you did and why it mattered to you.

**Question 10:** If you had a completely free day with no obligations, and you could work on anything you wanted, what would you choose to do? Be specific about the activity, not just "relax" or "hang out."

---

## **CATEGORY 4: VALUES UNDER PRESSURE (3 questions)**

**Question 11:** Describe a time when you had to choose between two things you cared about—maybe between studying for a test and helping a friend, between what your parents wanted and what you felt was right, or between something safe and something risky. What did you choose and why?

**Question 12:** Think about a decision you made that your friends or family didn't really understand or support. What was it, and why did you choose to do it anyway?

**Question 13:** Have you ever had to choose between doing what people expected of you and doing what you actually felt drawn to do? Describe what happened and how you made the decision.

---

## **CATEGORY 5: SUFFERING & LIMITATION (2 questions)**

**Question 14:** Describe a time when something you really wanted didn't work out—maybe you didn't get accepted somewhere, didn't make a team, or failed at something important. How did you respond? What did you do next?

**Question 15:** What's something that limits what you can do right now—maybe money, location, grades, family situation, or something else? How do you think about that limitation? Does it feel like something you need to overcome, or does it help you understand what path you should take?

---

## **CATEGORY 6: LEGACY & IMPACT (3 questions)**

**Question 16:** Imagine you're 40 years old and someone asks you, "What do you do?" How do you hope you'd answer? What kind of work do you hope you'll be doing, and why would it matter?

**Question 17:** If you could be really good at solving one specific problem in the world—not just talk about it, but actually make progress on it through your career—what problem would you choose? Why that one?

**Question 18:** Think about the kind of impact you want your life to have. When you're older, what do you want people to say about how your work affected them or made things better?

---

## **CATEGORY 7: CONTEXT & DIRECTION (2 questions)**

**Question 19:** What are you actually good at? Not what you wish you were good at, but what do people come to you for? What do teachers, friends, or family say you do well? Give specific examples.

**Question 20:** Right now, what majors or career paths are you considering (even if you're not sure)? What draws you to those options, and what makes you hesitate about them?

—----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **NOTES:**

### **What the AI Should Analyze:**

For each response, the model should identify:

1. **Service Orientation Patterns:**  
   * Direct care vs. systemic solutions  
   * People-focused vs. problem-focused  
   * Relational vs. technical helping  
   * Individual vs. community impact  
2. **Problem-Solving Patterns:**  
   * What kind of disorder compels them (injustice, inefficiency, suffering, absence of beauty, etc.)  
   * Scale of problems (individual, organizational, societal)  
   * Approach to solutions (direct service, policy, innovation, education)  
3. **Energy Sources:**  
   * Working with people, systems, ideas, or tangible things  
   * Creating, organizing, discovering, caring, teaching  
   * Solo vs. collaborative work  
   * Intellectual vs. hands-on engagement  
4. **Values & Decision-Making:**  
   * How they weigh duty, calling, risk, security  
   * Family/community responsibility in decisions  
   * Theological maturity in thinking about calling  
5. **Response to Obstacles:**  
   * Providence thinking vs. pure obstacle-overcoming  
   * Adaptability vs. perseverance  
   * How they interpret closed doors  
6. **Vision & Legacy:**  
   * Scope of impact (local vs. broad, immediate vs. generational)  
   * Focus on individuals, systems, culture, or ideas  
   * What "contribution" means to them

### **Expected Output:**

The model should map these patterns to the **17 vocational categories** and suggest specific career pathways with explanations based on their actual responses.

### **1\. SERVICE ORIENTATION**

**What it reveals:** *How* someone naturally serves others

**Why it's essential:**

* Reformed theology says all work is service to neighbor  
* But people serve in fundamentally different ways (direct care vs. building systems vs. creating beauty vs. teaching)  
* This reveals the **mode** of their calling

**What we learn:** Are they drawn to healing, teaching, creating, organizing, protecting, etc.?

---

### **2\. PROBLEM-SOLVING DRAW**

**What it reveals:** *What* disorder or need compels them

**Why it's essential:**

* Cultural mandate \= addressing disorder and cultivating creation  
* People are drawn to different kinds of brokenness (suffering, inefficiency, injustice, ugliness, ignorance)  
* This reveals the **domain** of their calling

**What we learn:** Are they compelled by physical suffering, systemic injustice, lack of beauty, intellectual confusion, environmental degradation, etc.?

---

### **3\. ENERGY & ENGAGEMENT**

**What it reveals:** Where their **gifts** actually lie (not just what they think they should do)

**Why it's essential:**

* Gifts are from God for service  
* Flow states reveal natural gifting  
* Energy vs. drain distinguishes calling from obligation  
* This reveals the **method** of their work

**What we learn:** Do they work with people, systems, ideas, or things? Creating, organizing, discovering, caring?

---

### **4\. VALUES UNDER PRESSURE**

**What it reveals:** How they actually make decisions when competing goods collide

**Why it's essential:**

* Luther emphasized vocation includes your "station" (family, community responsibilities)  
* Reformed theology recognizes calling isn't just about personal fulfillment  
* This reveals their **theological maturity** and how they navigate real constraints

**What we learn:** Do they honor family duty? Take risks for calling? Seek security? Follow conviction over approval?

---

### **5\. SUFFERING & LIMITATION**

**What it reveals:** How they interpret obstacles and closed doors

**Why it's essential:**

* Reformed theology has strong view of Providence  
* Closed doors can be guidance, not just obstacles  
* This reveals whether they see God's hand in circumstances  
* Shows adaptability vs. perseverance

**What we learn:** Do they see limitations as God's direction or just barriers? How do they discern Providence?

---

### **6\. LEGACY & IMPACT**

**What it reveals:** What they want to ultimately **contribute**

**Why it's essential:**

* Cultural mandate \= long-term cultivation  
* Reveals scope of calling (individual vs. systemic, local vs. broad)  
* Shows what "faithful service" means to them  
* This reveals the **scale and vision** of their calling

**What we learn:** Do they think about individuals helped, systems changed, beauty created, knowledge advanced, communities built?
